# What is an LLM Wiki?

> A living knowledge base you build alongside your exploration of Large Language Models.

---

## The Core Idea

An **LLM Wiki** is a personal (or shared) collection of interconnected notes about the field of Large Language Models. Unlike a static document or a bookmark list, a wiki grows organically — each new concept you learn becomes a node, and each connection you discover becomes an edge.

Think of it as your second brain for the AI era: you read a paper, watch a talk, or experiment with a model, and instead of the insight fading away, it gets captured, linked, and made findable forever.

---

## Why LLMs Specifically?

The LLM space moves fast — faster than almost any other field in technology right now. Without a system to capture and connect what you learn, you end up:

- Re-reading the same concepts every time you encounter them
- Forgetting context around *why* something matters
- Losing track of how ideas relate (e.g., how RLHF leads into Constitutional AI)
- Having no cumulative advantage from months of study

A wiki solves all of this. It compounds your understanding over time.

---

## What Goes in an LLM Wiki?

Your wiki can contain any of the following:

**Concepts** — Core ideas like attention, tokenization, embeddings, fine-tuning, RAG, RLHF, chain-of-thought prompting, constitutional AI, and more.

**Models** — Notes on specific models: GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Llama, Mistral — their architectures, capabilities, and release context.

**Papers** — Summaries and key takeaways from influential research (e.g., *Attention Is All You Need*, *InstructGPT*, *LIMA*).

**Tools & Frameworks** — LangChain, LlamaIndex, Hugging Face, Ollama — what they do and when to use them.

**Personal experiments** — Prompts that worked, outputs you found interesting, observations from running models locally.

**Daily discoveries** — Things you read, watched, or discussed that shifted your understanding.

---

## Wiki vs. Notes vs. Docs

| Format | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| **Wiki** | Interconnected, grows over time, browsable | Takes time to maintain |
| **Flat notes** | Fast to write | Hard to navigate, easy to forget |
| **Documentation** | Formal, shareable | Rigid, not personal |

A wiki sits in the sweet spot: structured enough to be useful, personal enough to stay current.

---

## The Network Graph Advantage

The most powerful feature of this wiki is the **concept map** — a visual graph where:

- Each node is a page (concept, model, tool, paper)
- Each edge is a connection ("uses", "builds on", "contrasts with", "enables")

When you look at the graph, you immediately see:
- Which concepts are most central (high connectivity)
- Which areas you've explored deeply vs. barely touched
- Unexpected paths between distant ideas

This is learning as navigation, not just recall.
